Switching to NERIS: The Checklist (No, You Don't Migrate From NFIRS)

Nine steps for getting your department's NERIS reporting up and running, in the order that actually makes sense to do them. None of them involve moving data out of NFIRS, because there's nothing left to move.

Updated July 2026 7 min read
Guide The Checklist NERIS Software
Before you start

There's no migration to run. NFIRS closed for good in February 2026, and there's no hand-off between it and NERIS, no export button, no data pipe. Setting up NERIS means starting a new incident reporting system, not moving one over. Work through this checklist in order: registration and reporting path first, then training and validation once entry is actually set up.

If you landed here searching for how to migrate from NFIRS to NERIS, here's the short version: there isn't a migration. NFIRS was a closed federal system, and whatever incident history your department has stays with USFA or whichever vendor ran your old records software. NERIS doesn't pull that history in, and it doesn't need to. What it needs is your department reporting new incidents going forward.

Starting fresh is the easy part, especially with the right software behind it. LadderOps, for example, sets a department up on NERIS by starting with a photo of the current roster and apparatus list, running AI-assisted setup from there, then entering each incident once with guided validation before it goes straight to NERIS. No parallel spreadsheet, no re-keying the same report twice.

Background on what NERIS is and why NFIRS went away is in our NERIS 2026 guide. This page is just the steps to get your department reporting.

The checklist

  1. 1

    Confirm your department's NERIS registration and entity ID

    Every department needs to be registered with NERIS to get its 10-digit entity ID, which replaces the old 5-digit FDID departments used under NFIRS. If you're not sure whether your department is registered, your state fire marshal's office or state NFIRS/NERIS program manager can confirm your status and walk you through registration if needed.

  2. 2

    Pick a reporting path: direct entry or a connected RMS

    Decide whether officers will enter incidents by hand directly into NERIS, or whether records management software submits on your behalf. If you're evaluating RMS options, ask the vendor point-blank whether they submit to NERIS through its API. If they don't, you're looking at manual double entry until that changes.

  3. 3

    Set up all-hazards categories, not just fire codes

    NERIS expects EMS, hazmat, rescue, and public-assist calls logged the same way structure fires are. If your crews are used to only doing paperwork for fire calls, this is the habit that has to change first, before the software setup matters much.

  4. 4

    Train officers and record-takers on the new entry workflow

    Whoever fills out incident reports, chief, secretary, or individual officers, needs a walkthrough of the new fields and categories before their first real call under the new system, not during it.

  5. 5

    Turn on validation before you submit, not after

    Submissions missing required fields or with inconsistent data get bounced back. Catching that at entry time, with guided validation, saves the follow-up work of chasing a rejected report down two weeks later.

  6. 6

    Submit a real incident and confirm it lands in NERIS

    Don't assume the connection works because setup went smoothly. Submit an actual report and check your NERIS dashboard to confirm it shows up before you trust the pipeline for real reporting.

  7. 7

    Assign one person to own NERIS reporting

    Somebody, chief, secretary, or records officer, needs to be the person who checks that reports are actually going through, not just that they were filled out. Reporting gaps are easy to miss until a state audit asks about them.

  8. 8

    Archive your old NFIRS records for your own files

    This is the one step that touches your old data, and it's about your own archive, not NERIS. NERIS doesn't import NFIRS history and doesn't need it. If your department kept records in its own RMS, that history is already yours. If you relied on eNFIRS alone and didn't export before the February 2026 shutdown, your state NFIRS program manager can tell you what's still recoverable through the NFIRS Public Data Release.

  9. 9

    Know who to call when something breaks

    Your state NFIRS/NERIS program manager for registration and entity ID questions. Your RMS vendor's support line for submission or validation errors. NERIS support, run through FSRI, for platform-level issues.

If you're starting from zero: steps 1 and 2 are the ones that block everything else. Get your entity ID confirmed and your reporting path picked before worrying about training or validation, those only matter once data actually has somewhere to go.

Frequently asked questions

Do we have to switch right now if we haven't already?

Yes. NFIRS closed to all users in February 2026. There's no fallback system left to report through. If your department hasn't set up NERIS reporting yet, you're already overdue, not early.

What if our current software isn't connected to NERIS?

Ask your vendor directly whether they submit to NERIS through its API. If they don't, and don't have a near-term plan to, you're looking at manual double entry into NERIS on top of your existing system, or switching to software that does connect.

Is there a cost to register for NERIS or submit incident data?

No. NERIS is free for fire departments, EMS agencies, and other emergency service agencies. Registration and submission carry no fee from USFA or FSRI. Any cost comes from the software or entry tool you use to get data into it.

What happens to our old NFIRS incident history?

It doesn't move anywhere automatically, and NERIS doesn't need it to start working. If your department kept its own records system, that history already lives with you. If you relied on eNFIRS alone and didn't export before the February 2026 shutdown, contact your state NFIRS program manager: some historical data is preserved in the NFIRS Public Data Release, but individual department exports are no longer available from USFA directly.
